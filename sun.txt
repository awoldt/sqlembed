The Solar System consists of the Sun and all objects that orbit it.

The Sun contains more than 99% of the mass in the Solar System and is classified as a G-type main-sequence star. Its immense gravity keeps planets, moons, asteroids, comets, and other bodies in orbit.

Mercury is the closest planet to the Sun and has extreme temperature variations due to its thin atmosphere.

Venus is similar in size to Earth but has a dense atmosphere primarily composed of carbon dioxide. Surface temperatures on Venus are hot enough to melt lead.

Earth is the third planet from the Sun and the only known world to support life. Approximately 71% of Earth's surface is covered by water.

Mars is often called the Red Planet because of iron oxide on its surface. Scientists continue to search for evidence of ancient microbial life on Mars.

Jupiter is the largest planet in the Solar System. It is a gas giant with dozens of moons and a massive storm known as the Great Red Spot.

Saturn is famous for its extensive ring system composed primarily of ice and rock particles.

Uranus rotates on its side compared to most planets, likely due to a collision early in its history.

Neptune is the farthest known major planet from the Sun and experiences some of the strongest winds in the Solar System.

Beyond Neptune lies the Kuiper Belt, a region containing icy bodies and dwarf planets such as Pluto.
